Welcome (test release 1.06)

'The Planets Online' is a fun way to learn about the planets, science, music, and much more - all while accompanied by an orchestra.

Click the Options button to choose from a number of different commentaries. As the music plays the comments will be updated and previews of related web pages will appear in this window.

A broad range of commentaries are coming soon, including:

  • About the Music - learn about the composer Gustav Holst and his inspiration for The Planets
  • About the (real) Planets - an introduction to the planets in our solar system
  • Science Sources - links to NASA, ESA, etc. showing the original images
  • Space Missions & Projects - about the spacecraft, telescopes, landers & rovers exploring our solar system
  • Meet the Scientists - the astronomers and technicians who made the images
  • Music Composition - Holst's techniques and innovations
  • Musical Performance - hear from musicians in each orchestra section
  • Performance Technology - how the visuals were performed live
  • Visual Story-telling - how the visuals were choreographed with the music
  • Trivia and ‘What’s wrong with this picture?’ - learn where 'artistic' images replaced real ones
  • Just for Kids

Requirements

The Planets Online makes extensive use of features in modern browsers, so please make sure yours is up to date.

For Teachers

This site can help students of all ages learn about a variety of subjects and includes features specifically for teachers. For example, you can send students special links that draw their attention to particular comments, images, and music selections. The following link shows web pages from NASA, so would be of interest to an astronomy class: theplanetsonline.com/?commentary=science-sources. And this next link would be suitable for a music class: theplanetsonline.com/?commentary=music. For more details see the Teacher’s Guide.

About the source material

While many of the visuals are original animations everything shown has some basis in fact and has as its source data from telescopes, orbiting spacecraft, or rovers on the planets' surfaces. Source images, video, and computer modeling courtesy of NASA, JPL-Caltech, SwRI, DLR, ESA, Johns Hopkins University Applied Physics Laboratory, Goddard Space Flight Center, The Space Telescope Science Institute, The Advanced Visualization Laboratory at the NCSA (esp. Drs. B. Robertson & L. Hernquist), Carnegie Institution of Washington, USGS, California Institute of Technology, Lunar & Planetary Institute (esp. Dr. P Schenk), Malin Space Science Systems, The Lunar and Planetary Laboratory at the University of Arizona (esp. Dr. P Smith) and the Institute of Geological Sciences at The Free University of Berlin. Special thanks to Bard Canning for the enhanced Mars descent, Arthur Lepage for 3D modeling, and Andy Ermolli for deep space astrophotography.
